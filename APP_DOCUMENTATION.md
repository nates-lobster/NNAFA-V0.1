# NeuroMemory: Complete System Documentation

Welcome to the NeuroMemory application! This document is designed to take you from a complete beginner to a full understanding of what this application is, how it works, what every graph means, and how data flows through the system.

---

## 1. High-Level Overview: What is this App?

NeuroMemory is a **Neurofeedback and Brainwave Analysis** application. It reads live electrical signals from the brain (via an EEG headset like the Muse), processes those signals to remove noise, and breaks them down into recognizable "brainwave" states (like focused, relaxed, or asleep). 

It is built on a **Hybrid Architecture**:
* **The Python Backend (The Brain):** Does all the heavy mathematical lifting. It connects to the headset, cleans the data, runs the complex math (Fourier Transforms, FIR filtering), and streams the clean results.
* **The C# Frontend (The Face):** A Windows user interface that takes the math from the backend and turns it into real-time graphs, audio feedback, and user controls.

These two sides talk to each other extremely fast using a network technology called **WebSockets**.

## 2. Navigating the UI: What Each Graph Shows

The application is divided into several visual sections (often organized as tabs or grouped panels). Here is what each one does:

### A. Real-Time EEG Telemetry (The Raw Data)
This section shows the live electrical signals coming from four sensors on the user's head.
* **TP9 & TP10 (Left & Right Ear):** Sensors behind the ears. Good for picking up general brain states, but highly sensitive to jaw clenching.
* **AF7 & AF8 (Left & Right Forehead):** Sensors on the forehead. Excellent for reading focus and eye movements.
* **What you see:** Two lines per graph. The **Gray line** is the raw signal straight from the headset (often messy). The **Blue line** is the filtered, cleaned-up signal.

### B. Power Spectrum / FFT (The Brainwave Mix)
Brain signals are actually a messy combination of many different frequencies happening all at once. The **FFT (Fast Fourier Transform)** graph takes the messy line and splits it into a bar/scatter graph showing *how much* of each frequency is present.
* **X-Axis (Horizontal):** The frequency in Hertz (Hz) from 0 to 40 Hz.
* **Y-Axis (Vertical):** The power/strength of that frequency.
* **The Color Bands:**
  * **Delta (1-4 Hz):** Deep sleep.
  * **Theta (4-8 Hz):** Drowsy, meditative, or deeply relaxed.
  * **Alpha (8-12 Hz):** Awake but relaxed (eyes closed).
  * **Beta (12-30 Hz):** Alert, focused, active thinking.
  * **Gamma (30-40 Hz):** High-level processing, sudden insight.

### C. Brainwave Trends
Instead of showing a split-second view, this graph tracks your brainwave powers *over time*. It acts like a scrolling heartbeat monitor for your mental states, drawing continuous lines for Delta, Theta, Alpha, Beta, and Gamma.

### D. Emulator vs. Software Validations
This section is mostly for developers. It isolates a single brainwave (e.g., just the Alpha wave). 
* **Emulator Plot:** Shows a perfect, mathematically generated wave.
* **Software Plot:** Shows how well the app's internal math was able to extract that specific wave from a noisy signal. If the math is working, these two lines should look identical.

## 3. The Debug Pipeline (How Data is Cleaned)

This is the most important tab for understanding how the app actually works. EEG data is incredibly fragile; blinking your eyes creates a massive electrical spike that drowns out brainwaves. The **Debug Pipeline** shows the 4 distinct stages data goes through to become usable:

* **Stage 1: Raw Signal (Gray)**
  * *What happens:* Data arrives from the headset. The app mathematically centers it on the graph (removes "DC offset").
  * *What it looks like:* Usually erratic, wandering up and down, and full of static.

* **Stage 2: Filtered (Blue)**
  * *What happens:* The backend applies "FIR Filters" to block useless data. It chops off anything below 1 Hz (body movement) and above 40 Hz (useless static). It also applies a "Notch Filter" at exactly 60Hz to delete the electrical hum generated by wall outlets and lights in your room.
  * *What it looks like:* A much smoother, more recognizable wavy line.

* **Stage 3: Denoised (Green)**
  * *What happens:* The "Artifact Rejection" stage. The backend looks at the filtered data and asks, "Did the user just blink or clench their jaw?" If the electrical spike is too high, it labels the data as an `ARTIFACT` and safely drops it so it doesn't ruin the session math. 
  * *What it looks like:* The exact same as Stage 2, except it instantly goes flat (zero) when you blink or move too much.

* **Stage 4: FFT Power Spectrum (Purple)**
  * *What happens:* Only the pristine, surviving data from Stage 3 makes it here. The clean data is broken apart into its base frequencies to calculate the Delta, Theta, Alpha, Beta, and Gamma numbers.

## 4. Settings & User Controls

How do the knobs and sliders affect the math described above?

* **Artifact Threshold (uV):** Controls Stage 3 of the pipeline. If set to `150`, any electrical wave larger than 150 microvolts is considered a "blink" or "muscle movement" and thrown away. Setting it lower means the app is stricter; setting it higher lets more noise through.
* **Low Cut & High Cut (Hz):** Controls Stage 2 of the pipeline. It sets the boundaries for the filters. Usually kept at 1Hz and 40Hz.
* **Global Gain:** Acts like a volume knob for the raw brain signal, multiplying the size of the waves so they are easier to see on graphs.
* **Target Ratio & Neurofeedback Audio:** The core feature of the app. You select a Numerator (e.g., Alpha) and Denominator (e.g., Beta). If your brain produces more Alpha than Beta, the ratio goes up. The app plays an audio file (like rainfall), and dynamically alters the audio volume based on how close you are to your "Target Ratio".
* **Calibrate Button:** Listens to your brain for 60 seconds to find your average baseline Ratio, setting a personalized target for your session.

## 5. Tools & Process Management

Because the app is a hybrid, the Frontend needs to launch and manage the Python backend.

* **Launch Engine:** Starts the real data-processing backend (`engine.py`). It will wait for a real headset to connect via LSL.
* **Launch Emulator:** Starts a "fake" headset (`emulator.py`). It generates perfect, artificial brainwaves so developers can test the graphs and math without wearing a real headset.
* **Launch BlueMuse:** Opens a 3rd party tool that physically bridges a Bluetooth Muse headset to your computer.
* **Launch LabRecorder:** Opens a tool to save your raw brain data to a file for later scientific research.
* **Kill All Python:** A panic button. If the app freezes or multiple invisible Python backends get stuck running in the background, this completely wipes them from the system memory.
